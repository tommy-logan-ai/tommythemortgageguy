What the Fed's April Decision Means for Michigan Home Buyers

April 30, 2026

The Headline

The Federal Reserve held its benchmark interest rate steady at 3.50–3.75% on April 29. The market had this outcome priced in at roughly 95% probability, so no surprise here. But what happens next — and what it means for you — is worth understanding.

Why Your Mortgage Rate Moved Today

The 10-year Treasury yield ticked up from 4.35% to 4.41% following the announcement. That's about 6 basis points of upward drift. Mortgage rate sheets opened slightly worse by day's end. Not catastrophic, but it's a headwind, not a tailwind.

The Bigger Story: No Rate Cuts in 2026

Here's what matters for your home-buying timeline: Markets have now priced out virtually all rate cuts for the rest of 2026. The Fed's own internal guidance at the last meeting suggested only one cut by year-end — and today's statement didn't budge that.

Why? Three factors are holding the Fed's hand:

  • Geopolitical risk: The Iran situation and energy price uncertainty mean inflation could spike unexpectedly. The Fed can't risk loosening until that clears.
  • Tariff uncertainty: Trade policy is still in flux. That affects how much inflation we see in Q3 and Q4.
  • A deeply divided Fed: Today's vote was 8–4, the most divided Fed vote since 1992. Three governors wanted even more hawkish language; one wanted a rate cut. A Fed that can't agree on direction is a Fed that moves slowly.

Translation: Interest rates are staying elevated longer than many buyers hoped.

What About the New Fed Chair?

Kevin Warsh was cleared by the Senate Banking Committee today. He's not a "rate-cutting" dove — he's market-friendly but hawkish on inflation. If you're waiting for the Fed chair to come in and slash rates, don't hold your breath.

Meanwhile, Jerome Powell is staying on past his May 15 term end. Last time a Fed chair did this: 1948. It signals stability and Fed independence from political pressure — good for markets, but not a sign rates are coming down soon.

What You Should Do

If you've been waiting for rates to drop: Stop waiting. Markets aren't pricing a meaningful rate cut until 2027 at the earliest. The cost of waiting is almost always higher than the cost of buying now and refinancing later if rates do improve.

If you're on the fence: This is the week to have a real conversation about your numbers. You can buy at today's rates and refinance when — and if — the Fed pivots. That's a better play than sitting on the sidelines hoping for a cut that may not come until next year.
